LevelSpend a level-spending retirement solver
If you are still working
  1. Enter your age now, planning end age (an age you are unlikely to outlive), and salary; then your starting portfolio and any legacy: the amount, in today's dollars, you want left over at the end age for heirs or bequests. Enter 0 to spend everything.
  2. Enter any Social Security and pension amounts in today's dollars, with the ages they begin. Enter 0 if none. A spouse's benefits go under Spouse; with the spouse's start age entered there, their benefit ages are the spouse's own ages. Two more streams on your own timeline are under More income in Advanced. Each stream can also be given a stop age if it ends before the end age; leave it blank to run to the end. Stop ages can represent benefits ending at an expected death, such as a pension without survivor benefits or the smaller of a couple's two Social Security checks. Spending itself stays level to the end age. If a pension pays a fixed amount with no inflation adjustment, see Advanced at the bottom of the panel.
  3. Set the expected long-run portfolio growth rate. This is a real (after-inflation) return: real returns have historically ranged from roughly 2% to 7% depending on the era and starting valuations. No one knows this rate in advance; try several values and see how much the results move.
  4. Under Decision variables, enter the retirement age you have in mind and the share of salary you currently save.
  5. Read the chart and stats: if spending is level (the spending line runs unbroken across the retirement age) and the portfolio lasts to the end age, the plan is internally consistent. If not, either click solve next to retirement age (latest age at which retirement spending stays at or below your working level) or solve next to savings rate (rate that makes your chosen retirement age work). Hover or tap any year on the chart for that year's detail: income, spending, and the portfolio withdrawal or contribution.
  6. Revisit once a year, or after any major change in your finances: update your age, portfolio value, and any other inputs that have changed (salary, Social Security estimate, pension), and re-check. The plan is meant to be recalculated, not set once. To avoid re-typing, copy scenario link (top right) makes a bookmarkable link containing your inputs; nothing is stored otherwise.
If you are already retired
  1. Set retirement age equal to age now, salary to 0, and savings rate to 0. The working-phase inputs become irrelevant.
  2. Enter your age now, planning end age, portfolio value, and any legacy (the amount you want left over at the end age).
  3. Enter Social Security and pension in today's dollars with their start ages, including benefits you have not claimed yet. For a benefit that has already started, enter your age now as its start age (any earlier age gives the same result; the tool pays no income before the retirement age, which in this setup is your age now). A spouse's benefits go under Spouse, with ages on the spouse's own timeline once their start age is entered. Two more streams on your own timeline are under More income in Advanced. A stop age ends a stream early; leave it blank to run to the end age.
  4. Set the portfolio growth rate as in step 3 opposite.
  5. Read Retirement spending in the stats row: the level annual amount (today's dollars, before tax) your portfolio plus future income can sustain through the end age.
  6. Each year, or after any major change in your finances, refresh all the inputs: age, portfolio value, and income estimates. The updated Retirement spending is your spending level for the year ahead.
If you are semi-retired
  1. The test: does your paycheck decide your spending, or do you decide your spending and draw on savings as needed? If the second, use this setup even though paychecks are still arriving.
  2. Follow the already-retired setup above: retirement age equal to age now, salary 0, savings rate 0.
  3. Enter your remaining salary as a pension slot (or an Other income slot under Advanced): amount per year, start age now, stop age at your last working year. Leave its inflation-adjusted box under Advanced checked (the default). To the tool, a paycheck for a few more years is just another income stream.
  4. The tool computes one level spending amount across both phases. In years when the paycheck falls short of it, the difference is a portfolio withdrawal; nothing extra to enter. Hover or tap the chart to see it year by year.
All amounts are in today's dollars and pre-tax: withdrawals from traditional (pre-tax) accounts are spendable only after income tax, which this tool does not model. It also does not model required minimum distributions, account types, or market volatility. It answers one question: given a steady real return, what level of real spending is consistent with your resources.
